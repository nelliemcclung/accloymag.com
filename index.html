Crossdreaming

I like how Nabokov understood premonition, tucked under his bedsheets and blankets, scribbling down his thoughts in index cards. For Nabokov, dreams are unmoored, flowing freely in time. Consider: You wake up, you check Twitter, you find out that an atmospheric sinkhole appeared under Nelson, British Columbia, killing four and blocking the only highway between Vancouver and the east. This leads you to remember that, during the night, you dreamed your car veered off the edge of a steep cliff during a heavy storm. In the mind of Nabokov, you do not see into the events of the future, but the opposite; the event happens, and then it comes to visit you in your bed.

 

It's 4:30 AM, and my ex puts in her earrings, so the holes don't close. Shortly before five, I drop her off at the business tower, where she climbs up the stairs and in towards her coffee job.

We do this every morning. The trek from our house to the LRT station is long and requires crossing the dark parking lot of a football stadium. My dad laughs at me when I tell him our routine and the reasons behind it, but I answer him, that's not fair, I'm happy to take the time. 

            My ex and I have a short exchange one evening while we wait for sleep:

            I don't think I'm a boy. I think I'm something else.

            She shifts her hand down under the covers.

            You have a dick, right?

            Most days, after locking the door behind me, I dump myself, a tangle of limb and hair and discontent, into the shower basin to let scalding water run me over and then down the drain. Make me clean, God, and I'll be yours for the rest of my life. Just for one day: Make me young again, make me new, make me whole. Then I drag my body out of the bathroom and into bed with the lights still on. 

            Lucid dreams occur when dreamers become aware that they are dreaming. During my daily psychic exercise, dripping wet, naked, clammy with sweat and splayed out on the bed from edge to edge, I come home to myself.

 

I've been describing my habit, but dreams are particular experiences, so follow me, yes, that's it, come closer, right up against the grit of my story. Knock, and the door of my childhood house swings open. Sit and breathe the salacious air of home alone. Dead static plays on the television. The woodstove is lit, add another log to the fire, more warmth for winter. 

            No, but this is a dream.           

            You reach into the pocket of your homework desk and retrieve your lip gloss, your creams, your powders. You move, and the world moves with you. 

            No, but this is a dream.

            You hear joyful shouts from the kitchen. You cut the cake, Happy Birthday, Chrissy! The guests go, the girls and the boys. Your father passes you another slice, and you eat the half without the icing. 

            No, but this is a dream.
             You're nineteen, and you're in the bedroom of your student accommodation. You're watching dumb YouTube videos on your Macbook with the guy who sits beside you in your Astronomy class, the one with the generous laugh. You feel the twitch of his steady hand.

            No, but this is a dream.

            You're twenty-four, now, you have a room of your own in your house in the city. You put the coffee on, you put your hair up, you put your earrings in, so that the holes don't close. You say hi to your roommate, and she greets you back. A dog-eared copy of Pale Fire lies on the dining room table.
